Everything You Need to Know About the Microbiome

Author — June-sang Yang, family physician

Overview

A reference that begins by dividing anaerobic bacteria into the four major classes GPAC / GPAB / GNAC / GNAB by Gram stain + morphology (cocci/bacilli) and organizing each group's representative species, phylum, associated diseases, potentially contaminated foods, and antibiotic susceptibility. From there it expands to facultative anaerobes, in-depth antibiotic comparisons (with exception organisms such as Actinomyces·Aggregatibacter), halitosis (VSC producers, substrate foods) and H₂S pathways and SIBO. It further covers the links between the gut microbiome and neurodegenerative and psychiatric disease (the gut–brain axis, depression), microbial drug metabolism (e.g., Eggerthella inactivating digoxin/levodopa), and a critical deconstruction of dogmas such as "more colon diversity = health" and "F/B ratio = obesity." In short, an anaerobe-4-class backbone expanded into a clinical reference spanning antibiotics, halitosis, systemic disease, and microbiome literacy.

Share of the 4 major classes in the healthy adult gut

More than 99% of gut bacteria are anaerobic, and at the phylum level Bacillota (formerly Firmicutes) + Bacteroidota (formerly Bacteroidetes) account for ~90%. Converting this into the four major Gram/morphology classes (by relative abundance):

Class Representative genera Main phylum Approximate range
GPAB (Gram+ bacilli) Faecalibacterium·Clostridium·Eubacterium·Roseburia·Bifidobacterium Bacillota + Actinomycetota ~50–75% (most dominant)
GNAB (Gram– bacilli) Bacteroides·Prevotella·Fusobacterium Bacteroidota + Fusobacteriota ~15–40% (2nd)
GPAC (Gram+ cocci) Peptostreptococcus·Peptoniphilus·Finegoldia·Ruminococcus (some) Bacillota ~1–5% (minority)
GNAC (Gram– cocci) Veillonella·Megasphaera·Acidaminococcus Bacillota (Negativicutes) ~<1–2% (very small)

Caveats (why the ranges are wide) - Individual variation / F/B ratio fluctuation: the Bacillota/Bacteroidota ratio varies greatly with diet, age, and region → wide ranges. - Culture vs metagenomics: old culture data and 16S/shotgun sequencing estimates differ. The values above are by relative abundance. - GPAB dominance: the main butyrate producers (Faecalibacterium·Roseburia·Clostridium clusters) are concentrated here, taking more than half. - Cocci (GPAC·GNAC) are a minority: even combined they are usually around 5%. Veillonella is common but low in abundance. - GNAC pitfall (reconfirmed): it stains Gram-negative but phylogenetically belongs to Bacillota (Negativicutes).

Key insights of the phylogenetic axis - Anaerobic Gram-positive bacteria (GPAC+GPAB) are effectively either Bacillota or Actinomycetota — cocci and Clostridium are Bacillota; filamentous forms (Actinomyces), Cutibacterium, and Bifidobacterium are Actinomycetota (high-G+C Gram-positives). - Anaerobic Gram-negative bacilli (GNAB) are either Bacteroidota (the Bacteroides lineage) or Fusobacteriota (Fusobacterium) — different phyla. - The GNAC pitfall: outwardly Gram-negative but phylogenetically Bacillota (Firmicutes). Thin peptidoglycan, presence/absence of LPS, etc. make them stain negative, but evolutionarily they are class Negativicutes within the Gram-positive lineage. - Facultatively anaerobic / microaerophilic Gram-negatives (Enterobacteriaceae·Aggregatibacter·Campylobacter) are mostly Pseudomonadota (Proteobacteria).

Deep dive — Finegoldia magna: the clinically representative GPAC pathogen

GPAC constitute the largest anaerobic group, accounting for about 25–30% of all anaerobes isolated from clinical specimens, and among them Finegoldia magna (formerly Peptostreptococcus magnus) is the most commonly isolated and most virulent species (Neumann et al., Front Microbiol 2020; Boyanova et al., 2016, PMID 27756620). Although it is normal flora of the skin and mucosa, it becomes a prominent opportunistic pathogen once host defenses are breached.

Major infections

  • Skin/soft tissue infections (SSTI), diabetic foot ulcers
  • Orthopedic implant-associated infection — a cause of slow-growing, low-virulence delayed infection (J Clin Microbiol 2017, 00866-17)
  • Osteomyelitis, septic arthritis
  • The most commonly isolated anaerobe in non-puerperal breast abscess
  • Female genital tract infections such as pelvic infection and endometritis

Virulence factors

Factor Function
Protein L Binds immunoglobulin light chains (κ) → acts as a B-cell superantigen, disrupting host antibody defenses
Protein FAF (Finegoldia adhesion factor) Promotes surface adhesion and bacterial aggregation, aiding tissue colonization
SufA (a subtilisin-family serine protease) Degrades the host antimicrobial peptide LL-37 → evasion of innate immunity
Neutrophil activation Induces ROS (reactive oxygen species) bursts and NET release → amplifies local inflammation (Neumann 2020)

Antibiotic susceptibility — generally susceptible (S) to β-lactams (penicillin, ampicillin), metronidazole, vancomycin, and linezolid. However, clinical isolates report clindamycin resistance of ~9.5% and penicillin resistance of ~2.4% (Ann Clin Microbiol Antimicrob, PMC10127037), and multidrug-resistant strains are gradually emerging, so susceptibility testing is recommended in severe and implant-associated infections. Older-generation FQs such as ciprofloxacin are unsuitable (R).

➕ Eggerthella lenta — a drug-metabolizing Gram-positive anaerobic bacillus (GPAB, Actinomycetota)

A Gram-positive, obligate-anaerobic, non-spore-forming bacillus. Phylum Actinomycetota / family Eggerthellaceae (formerly Coriobacteriaceae) — a GPAB in the same phylum as Actinomyces·Cutibacterium above. A commensal of the gut, oral cavity, and urogenital tract.

  • Clinical — high-mortality anaerobic bacteremia: an increasingly recognized cause of anaerobic bloodstream infection. ~33% 1-year mortality (20–40% across reports), usually of GI origin (appendicitis/perforation) and polymicrobial. ⚠️ Antibiotic pitfall: high MICs to piperacillin-tazobactam (TZP) — empiric TZP monotherapy is associated with increased mortality → amox-clav, cefoxitin, metronidazole, and carbapenems are reliable options.
  • ★ Drug metabolism — digoxin inactivation: via the cgr operon (cgr1/cgr2, cardiac glycoside reductase) it reduces digoxin to inactive dihydrodigoxin → reduced digoxin efficacy in some patients. Arginine (a high-protein diet) inhibits this reaction, and strain-level presence/absence of cgr explains the variability (Haiser·Turnbaugh, Science 2013).
  • ★ Drug metabolism — levodopa (Parkinson's drug): after Enterococcus faecalis decarboxylates L-DOPA → dopamine, E. lenta dehydroxylates dopamine → m-tyramine via a molybdenum-dependent dopamine dehydroxylase → reduced levodopa bioavailability and variable patient response (Maini Rekdal·Balskus, Science 2019). Connects to the gut–brain / gut–drug axes in the later neurodegeneration and psychiatric sections.
  • ★ Psychiatric association: one of the most consistently increased genera in depression microbiome studies — elevated across MDD, bipolar, and schizophrenia in multiple meta-analyses, positively correlated with depression/anxiety/stress scores, with Mendelian randomization hinting at a causal link to MDD. Classed among pro-inflammatory taxa and also linked to antidepressant non-response.

5 major associated diseases

  1. Gas gangrene / food poisoning (C. perfringens)
  2. Pseudomembranous colitis (C. difficile)
  3. Botulism (C. botulinum)
  4. Actinomycosis / cervical abscess (Actinomyces)
  5. Acne / implant and shunt infection (C. acnes)

※ Note: C. tetani → tetanus

+ Desulfovibrio spp. (sulfate-reducing bacteria, SRB) — being Gram-negative, obligate anaerobic curved rods, they belong to GNAB. However, their lineage is Thermodesulfobacteriota (the old Proteobacteria Deltaproteobacteria), different from the two phyla above, and their pathogenic mechanism is not amino-acid degradation but dissimilatory sulfate reduction (SO₄²⁻→H₂S), so they are treated separately from the 3 GNAB genera commonly dealt with clinically (Bacteroides·Prevotella·Fusobacterium). They inhabit the gut, periodontal pockets, and tongue coating, contributing to halitosis and gut mucosal damage through H₂S production (same phylum as Bilophila wadsworthia).

➕ Bilophila wadsworthia — a bile-loving, H₂S-producing anaerobe (a cousin of Desulfovibrio)

A Gram-negative, obligate-anaerobic, bile-resistant ("Bilophila" = bile-loving), asaccharolytic bacillus in the same family Desulfovibrionaceae / phylum Thermodesulfobacteriota as Desulfovibrio above. It does not reduce sulfate; instead it uses taurine and other organosulfonates as respiratory substrates to make H₂S (see the "three H₂S-production pathways" deep dive later for the pathway details).

  • Typical infections: appendicitis — the 3rd most common anaerobe in perforated/gangrenous appendicitis specimens (originally isolated at the Wadsworth VA). Also intra-abdominal abscess and, rarely, bacteremia/hepatic abscess (higher risk with intra-abdominal malignancy, abdominal surgery, transplant).
  • Diet–bile–colitis axis: saturated (milk) fat → more hepatic taurine-conjugated bile acid (taurocholic acid) → supplies organic sulfur → B. wadsworthia blooms → H₂S (genotoxic) → a TH1 response → colitis in genetically susceptible hosts (Il10⁻/⁻) (Devkota, Nature 2012). H₂S is also linked to IBD and colon cancer.
  • Other associations (limited/emerging evidence): colorectal cancer (H₂S genotoxicity), acute pancreatitis (gut–pancreas axis; enriched in AP patients + worsens pancreatitis in mice), and Parkinson's disease (gut–brain axis; enriched in PD gut, linked to basal-ganglia atrophy) — all at the association/preclinical level, not established causation.

5 major associated diseases

  1. Intra-abdominal abscess / peritonitis (B. fragilis) — the most common clinical anaerobic infection
  2. Aspiration pneumonia / lung abscess (Prevotella, Fusobacterium)
  3. Periodontitis (P. gingivalis)
  4. Lemierre's syndrome (suppurative thrombophlebitis of the internal jugular vein) (F. necrophorum)
  5. Bacteremia/sepsis and postoperative wound infection

⭐ Fusobacterium nucleatum and its link to colorectal cancer (CRC)

F. nucleatum (Fusobacteriota, GNAB), an oral commensal anaerobe, has recently drawn attention as a tumor-promoting bacterium in colorectal cancer (CRC).

  • Tumor-tissue-specific enrichment: F. nucleatum DNA is detected significantly higher in CRC tissue than in adjacent normal mucosa (Kostic 2012·Castellarin 2012, Genome Res), and it has been reported to co-metastasize to the tumor site and even liver metastatic lesions.
  • Adhesion/invasion — FadA adhesin: FadA binds E-cadherin on colonic epithelium → activating β-catenin/Wnt signaling, promoting cell proliferation and tumor growth (Rubinstein 2013, Cell Host Microbe).
  • Immune evasion — Fap2 lectin: Fap2 binds tumor-cell Gal-GalNAc to colonize the tumor, while simultaneously binding the inhibitory receptor TIGIT on NK cells and T cells to suppress antitumor immunity (Gur 2015, Immunity).
  • Shaping the tumor microenvironment: reported to recruit tumor-infiltrating myeloid cells, induce inflammatory cytokines, and confer chemotherapy resistance (5-FU/oxaliplatin resistance via the autophagy pathway) (Yu 2017, Cell).
  • Clinical implications: a high intratumoral F. nucleatum burden is associated with poor prognosis and recurrence. Fecal detection is being studied as a candidate non-invasive CRC screening biomarker. Causality is not yet established (evidence favors association/promotion; whether it is a "driver" remains debated).

Summary: F. nucleatum is best understood not as a sole cause of CRC but as an oncomicrobe that accelerates tumor progression through adhesion (FadA), immune evasion (Fap2), and chemoresistance within an already-formed tumor microenvironment.

⭐ Porphyromonas gingivalis and systemic disease — diabetes, fatty liver, vascular disease

P. gingivalis (Bacteroidota, GNAB), the key pathogen of periodontitis, is a "keystone pathogen" that spreads systemically beyond the periodontal pocket via bacteremia, virulence factors, and oral ingestion, and is bidirectionally entangled with the metabolic and vascular diseases below. Its common virulence factors are gingipains (Rgp/Kgp — cysteine proteases), fimbriae (FimA), and LPS, and outer membrane vesicles (OMVs) that carry bacterial products serve as the vehicle for long-range delivery.

① Type 2 diabetes — a bidirectional relationship - Periodontitis and type 2 diabetes are well established as a bidirectional association that worsens each other. Periodontal treatment significantly improves HbA1c. - Mechanism: P. gingivalis-derived gingipain directly proteolytically cleaves the insulin receptor (IR) → blocking insulin signaling → causing insulin resistance (Liu 2024, PMC11291925). Systemic low-grade inflammation (TNF-α·IL-6) from LPS/bacteremia worsens both insulin resistance and pancreatic β-cell dysfunction (Jia 2023, PMC10373507).

② Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD/NASH) — the oral–gut–liver axis - P. gingivalis is swallowed and reaches the GI tract, causing gut dysbiosis and increased intestinal permeability → allowing bacteria/LPS to enter the liver via the portal vein ("oral-gut-liver axis"). - In animal models, oral/periodontal infection worsens hepatic steatosis, inflammation, and fibrosis (NASH) (PMC5839206), promotes hepatocyte fat accumulation via CD36–PPARγ axis upregulation (high-fat-diet mice), and is further reported as a risk factor for liver injury via the ferroptosis (iron-dependent cell death) pathway (J Microbiol Immunol Infect series, S1286457922001101).

③ Cardiovascular / atherosclerosis - P. gingivalis DNA/antigen is detected in atheromas, reaching the vessel wall via bacteremia. - Mechanism: FimA and gingipain adhere to and invade vascular endothelial cells and macrophages → promoting endothelial dysfunction, foam cell formation, and inflammatory atheroma progression. Systemic inflammation from chronic bacteremia raises the risk of atherosclerosis and coronary artery disease (periodontitis–ASCVD epidemiologic association).

Summary: P. gingivalis is understood not to stop at local periodontal infection but to be a representative bacterium linking to systemic disease, promoting and worsening insulin resistance (diabetes), hepatic steatosis and fibrosis (NAFLD/NASH), and atherosclerosis (vascular disease) via gingipain·FimA·LPS·OMVs (some causality is at the animal-model/epidemiologic level, still being established).

The big picture: the gut is overwhelmingly an anaerobic world

⚠️ Pathogenicity of Lactobacillus — the exception to "lactobacilli are harmless"

Lactobacilli are very low-virulence commensals/probiotics (GRAS), but once isolated from a sterile site (blood, endocardium, CSF) they become opportunistic pathogens.

  • Typical infections: bacteremia and infective endocarditis (especially L. rhamnosus·L. casei).
  • Prognosis / epidemiology: a Finnish (Helsinki) cohort of 89 cases had 26% 1-month and 48% 1-year mortality (Salminen, CID 2004); a separate Taiwanese cohort of 89 cases was reported in which only half received appropriate antibiotics. The high mortality largely reflects severe underlying disease rather than the organism's own virulence — hence the "pathogen or prognostic marker" debate.
  • Risk groups: severe underlying disease/immunosuppression (diabetes, cancer, chemotherapy, transplant, HIV, steroids), ICU/central venous catheter (CVC), structural heart disease/prosthetic valve, decompensated cirrhosis, poor dental hygiene, and excessive probiotic intake.
  • ⚠️ Treatment key — intrinsic vancomycin resistance: most Lactobacillus are intrinsically vancomycin-resistant (susceptible in only ~22.5% in one review). First line = penicillin/ampicillin (± gentamicin synergy); alternatives clindamycin, moxifloxacin, daptomycin. Combine with source control such as central-line removal.
  • ⚠️ D-lactic acidosis (a non-infectious complication): with carbohydrate malabsorption (short bowel syndrome, post-bariatric surgery), colonic Lactobacillus and others over-ferment carbohydrate to D-lactate → high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis + episodic neurologic symptoms (confusion, ataxia, slurred speech, a "drunken" appearance). ⚠️ The routine lactate assay measures only L-lactate and can read normal, so a specific D-lactate assay is needed (diagnostic pitfall).
  • Links to the "rarely infects" note in the table above — rare, but when it happens, remember the vancomycin trap.

Key points distinguishing "anaerobe vs facultative anaerobe" (linked to the 4 major classes above)

  • Obligate anaerobe: oxygen = poison. Bacteroides, Clostridium, Fusobacterium, etc. → the subject of this document's 4 major classes (GPAC/GPAB/GNAC/GNAB).
  • Facultative anaerobe: respires with oxygen, ferments without → the Enterobacteriaceae/enterococci in the table above → outside the 4 major classes. Easy to culture (ordinary aerobic media) and commonly isolated.
  • Microaerophilic: prefers low oxygen concentration → Campylobacter, Helicobacter, and the Aggregatibacter discussed earlier.
  • Aerotolerant: cannot respire with oxygen but tolerates it → Lactobacillus, and exceptionally Actinomyces.

Why this distinction matters clinically

  • Culture: facultative anaerobes grow well in ordinary aerobic blood media → no special anaerobic culture needed as with obligate anaerobes. Rapidly detected in clinical specimens.
  • Empirical antibiotics: in polymicrobial infections mixing "anaerobes + facultative anaerobes", such as intra-abdominal infection, both must be covered → e.g., a 3rd-generation cephalosporin/carbapenem for facultatively anaerobic Gram-negatives (Enterobacteriaceae), and metronidazole for obligate anaerobes → hence the logic of "cephalosporin + metronidazole" or a single broad-spectrum agent (piperacillin-tazobactam, carbapenem).
  • Metronidazole is ineffective against facultative anaerobes (nitro-reduction fails in the presence of oxygen) → not used for Enterobacteriaceae/enterococcal infection. This extends the logic seen earlier with Actinomyces·Aggregatibacter.

Deep dive: Are penicillin/β-lactams "effective against almost all anaerobes"? — detailed comparison of 4 agents

Verdict: the terminology must be distinguished

  • "β-lactam/BLI combinations (amox-clav, pip-tazo)·carbapenems cover almost all anaerobes" → ✅ true
  • "Penicillin alone is effective against almost all anaerobes" → ❌ false
  • Penicillin G/ampicillin alone is ineffective against a large fraction of Gram-negative anaerobic bacilli (GNAB). Lumping penicillin together with "β-lactam/BLI combinations" causes misunderstanding.

Key mechanism: β-lactamase

Almost the entire Bacteroides fragilis group, and a large fraction of Prevotella·Porphyromonas, produce β-lactamase (cepA, cfiA, etc.) → cleaving penicillin's β-lactam ring and inactivating it. - Penicillin alone → resistant (R) to GNAB (especially B. fragilis) - Amoxicillin + clavulanate → clavulanate inhibits β-lactamase → susceptible again (S)

Deep dive: Does metronidazole work against "almost all anaerobes"? — anaerobes where it weakens or fails

The big picture

Metronidazole is the strongest card against typical (strict) anaerobes, especially Gram-negative anaerobic bacilli (Bacteroides·Prevotella·Fusobacterium), with almost no resistance (≈0–2%). But the qualifier "almost" is key — there is clearly a group that is structurally unresponsive or has reduced susceptibility.

Why it fails — the activation mechanism is key

Metronidazole is itself an inactive prodrug. The bacterium's internal low-redox anaerobic metabolism (pyruvate:ferredoxin oxidoreductase, PFOR, etc.) must reduce the nitro group (-NO₂) for it to become the active metabolite that damages DNA. → The stronger the strict anaerobic metabolism, the better it works; if the organism is aerotolerant/microaerophilic and this reduction mechanism is weak, the drug is not activated and is ineffective. This is the common cause of the "gaps" below.

Lactobacillus (many) GPAB a large fraction intrinsically R (especially aerotolerant species)

→ Pattern: aerotolerant members among the Gram-positive "bacilli (GPAB)" are metronidazole's main blind spot. The key contrast is that it is strongest against GNAB (Gram-negative bacilli) yet vulnerable against some GPAB.

③ Does metronidazole work well on GPAC (Gram-positive anaerobic cocci)? → mostly YES (but not 100%)

  • Multicenter studies combined: metronidazole resistance ~0–10%.
  • UK/Wales sentinel study: 0% resistance to metronidazole in GPAC (on par with piperacillin/tazobactam·imipenem)
  • Turkish university hospital: metronidazole and tigecycline showed high in vitro activity → suitable for empirical treatment
  • FEMS review: GPAC metronidazole resistance around 5–10% (favorable compared with penicillin 7–10%, clindamycin 7–20%)
  • Conclusion: metronidazole works fairly well on GPAC and is suitable as empirical treatment. However:
  • species-level variation exists (especially a few resistant strains of Parvimonas micra·some Peptoniphilus·Finegoldia)
  • this matches exactly the "S¹" footnote in the top table (some Finegoldia·Peptoniphilus may be R)
  • it is not "nearly 100%" the way it is for GNAB, so for empirical treatment of deep infections it is safer to supplement with a β-lactam/BLI combination

One-line summary

  • Metronidazole's strongest domain: Gram-negative anaerobic bacilli (GNAB, Bacteroides etc.) — resistance ≈0%
  • Works fairly well: GPAC (Gram-positive anaerobic cocci) — resistance 0–10%, empirically suitable but not 100%
  • Structural blind spot: aerotolerant Gram-positive bacilli — Actinomyces·Cutibacterium acnes·Lactobacillus·Bifidobacterium (R due to absent nitro-reduction mechanism)
  • Unsuitable: microaerophilic Streptococcus (S. anginosus group, etc.) — not a true anaerobe
  • Practical: understand metronidazole as "dedicated to Gram-negative anaerobes", and fill the Gram-positive coverage (including actinomycetes) with a β-lactam (amox-clav etc.) combination as the standard.

Deep dive: Actinomyces — the classic "anaerobe that metronidazole doesn't work on"

Identity: an anaerobic–microaerophilic Gram-positive filamentous bacillus

  • Gram-positive bacillus (GPAB), with a filamentous branching morphology → looks fungus-like in tissue but is a bacterium (non-acid-fast; differentiating point from Nocardia: Nocardia is partially acid-fast and aerobic).
  • Anaerobic to microaerophilic — this "aerotolerant" character is the clinical key.
  • Normal flora of the oral cavity, GI tract, and genitourinary tract. Low virulence, so it invades only when there is mucosal injury + tissue necrosis.
  • Representative species: Actinomyces israelii. Often a polymicrobial infection — found together with companion bacteria (e.g., Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans).

Why metronidazole doesn't work (linked to the previous section)

Metronidazole is a prodrug activated by strict anaerobic metabolism (nitro-group reduction), but Actinomyces is aerotolerant/microaerophilic, so this reduction mechanism is weak → the drug is not activated → resistant (R). Thus it is the classic exception to the formula "anaerobe = metronidazole".

The 3 major clinical forms (by site of involvement)

Cervicofacial ("lumpy jaw") 50–70% After dental infection, extraction, or oral trauma. A firm mass of the jaw/neck → chronic progression

Characteristic findings

  • Sulfur granules: yellow, round ≤1mm granules visible to the naked eye in drained pus = bacterial colony aggregates. However, not specific to Actinomyces — also seen in Nocardia and botryomycosis (Splendore-Hoeppli phenomenon).
  • Chronic/subacute course: contiguous spread ignoring fascial planes, with multiple abscesses + sinus tracts → frequently confused with tumor or fungal infection.
  • Diagnosis is by culture (fastidious, anaerobic, prolonged) + tissue biopsy. Early misdiagnosis is common.

Treatment — penicillin first-line, long-term high-dose

  • First-line: penicillin G or amoxicillin, high-dose and long-term. For tissue/abscess penetration, 6–12 months (may be shortened to 3 months with optimal surgical resection).
  • Some cases report penicillin G 12 MU/day + metronidazole combination, but metronidazole is ineffective against Actinomyces itself — this combination targets the accompanying anaerobes (GNAB etc.) of the polymicrobial infection, not the actinomycete itself.
  • Pelvic form (IUD-related): IUD removal + penicillin. Drain large abscesses.
  • Penicillin-allergy alternatives: doxycycline, clindamycin, ceftriaxone, erythromycin/clarithromycin (Actinomyces is generally macrolide-susceptible), etc.
  • Surgery (abscess drainage, debridement of necrotic tissue, sinus tract excision) combined with antibiotics shortens treatment duration and reduces recurrence.

One-line summary

  • Actinomyces = an anaerobic–microaerophilic Gram-positive filamentous bacillus, oral commensal, invades on mucosal injury → chronic mass, sinus tracts, sulfur granules.
  • Metronidazole R (aerotolerant mechanism) → the classic exception to the "if anaerobe then metro" formula.
  • Treatment = high-dose penicillin/amoxicillin long-term (6–12 months) + surgery if needed; IUD removal for the pelvic form.
  • Related: directly linked to footnotes ①·② in the top table (metro is ineffective against Actinomyces·Cutibacterium) and to the "GPAB" class.

Deep dive: Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans — the Gram-negative "companion" of Actinomyces

Identity: the name looks like an actinomycete but it's Gram-negative — a microaerophile outside the 4 major classes

  • Phylogeny: Pseudomonadota (formerly Proteobacteria), class Gammaproteobacteria, family Pasteurellaceae. ⚠️ The "actinomyceto-" in the name only means it coexists with Actinomyces (phylum Actinomycetota); its lineage is a completely different phylum.
  • A Gram-negative coccobacillus. Its old name Actinobacillus actinomycetemcomitans → the species epithet "actinomycetemcomitans" (accompanying the actinomycetes) derives from being frequently co-isolated with Actinomyces in actinomycosis lesions.
  • Microaerophilic/capnophilic (requires CO₂), fastidious (hard to culture) — not a true anaerobe → treated as a separate organism outside this document's 4 major classes (GPAC/GPAB/GNAC/GNAB).
  • The "A" of the HACEK group: Haemophilus/Aggregatibacter → Aggregatibacter·Cardiobacterium·Eikenella·Kingella. The leading cause of Gram-negative endocarditis (up to ~5% of native valve endocarditis; HACEK as a whole ~10% of IE).
  • Oral commensal → invades through mucosal injury and periodontal tissue.

Relationship to halitosis — not a direct producer, but the upstream pathogen that "sets the stage"

The chemical reality of halitosis is volatile sulfur compounds (VSC) — hydrogen sulfide (H₂S)·methyl mercaptan (CH₃SH)·dimethyl sulfide ((CH₃)₂S) — with H₂S and CH₃SH making up about 90% of total VSC. Aa is not a main direct producer of these VSCs.

  • Direct contribution (weak): in the "most active VSC producers" table of a review synthesizing 40 years and 106 papers (Lee & Hong, Front Oral Health 2023;4:1229145), Aggregatibacter is not included. The representative strong H₂S producers are Fusobacterium·Neisseria·Porphyromonas; for CH₃SH they are Atopobium·Megasphaera·Prevotella·Selenomonas·Veillonella. The true VSC leads are the red complex (P. gingivalis·T. denticola·T. forsythia) and the Fusobacterium·Bacteroides·Peptostreptococcus lineages.
  • Indirect contribution (substantial): Aa is a representative pathogen of localized aggressive periodontitis (LAP), disrupting host immunity with leukotoxin (LtxA)·CDT (cytolethal distending toxin) and destroying periodontal tissue. This destruction creates the anaerobic habitat of deep periodontal pockets, setting the stage for the true VSC producers (red complex·F. nucleatum) to flourish. With at least one pocket >5mm deep, VSC values increase about 30% versus normal.
  • Vicious cycle: CH₃SH has high oral-mucosal permeability (more cytotoxic than H₂S), accelerating periodontitis → which in turn deepens pockets and increases VSC. Aa sits upstream in this cycle.
  • Epidemiologic marker: in tongue-coating microbiota comparison studies, the halitosis group showed no phylum-level difference but a significant increase at the genus/species level of Aggregatibacter·Campylobacter·Capnocytophaga·Prevotella·Treponema. The same-genus Aggregatibacter segnis is also reported as a tongue-coating-associated species.

Antibiotic susceptibility — clarithromycin/macrolides are "conditionally effective"

  • The strength of macrolides (azithromycin·clarithromycin) = killing intracellularly invasive bacteria.
  • A. actinomycetemcomitans invades the gingival epithelium and tissue, and azithromycin accumulates well in many mammalian cells, killing even bacteria hidden inside tissue/cells (the Azithromycin Kills Invasive A. actinomycetemcomitans study). An advantage over β-lactams, which cannot reach intracellular bacteria.
  • High periodontal-tissue concentration makes it useful in local/adjunctive treatment.
  • However, in vitro susceptibility is variable — not "always S".
  • Some oral-isolate studies observed intermediate resistance to azithromycin·clindamycin → efficacy may be limited if relied on alone.
  • That is, there is individual/strain variation, so macrolides are positioned as an adjunct/alternative to standard combination therapy rather than monotherapy.
  • Standard periodontitis regimen = amoxicillin + metronidazole combination (in addition to mechanical treatment such as scaling/root planing).
  • Why combined? A. actinomycetemcomitans is microaerophilic, so metronidazole alone is insufficient → amoxicillin is the mainstay, metronidazole covers the accompanying anaerobes. The synergy of the two raises the eradication rate.
  • When β-lactamase-producing strains are present, use amoxicillin-clavulanate.
  • Endocarditis (HACEK) = ceftriaxone first-line (3rd-generation cephalosporin), IV for 4–6 weeks. Ampicillin (if susceptibility confirmed)·fluoroquinolone as alternatives. Because of possible β-lactamase production, avoid empirical penicillin/ampicillin monotherapy.
  • Other: doxycycline/tetracycline S (periodontal local/systemic), fluoroquinolone S.

Ciprofloxacin/fluoroquinolone — active against Aggregatibacter (the exact opposite of anaerobes)

  • Unlike anaerobes, HACEK organisms (including Aggregatibacter) are generally fluoroquinolone-susceptible (S). Ciprofloxacin·levofloxacin·moxifloxacin are all active.
  • Endocarditis alternative: when a β-lactam (ceftriaxone) cannot be used (allergy, etc.), FQs such as ciprofloxacin are accepted as an alternative option (presuming confirmed susceptibility).
  • But ceftriaxone remains first-line — FQs are an alternative. In periodontitis, too, the standard is amox+metro and FQs are not usually first-line.
  • Key contrast: even among Gram-negatives, cipro is ineffective (R) against anaerobes (Bacteroides etc.) but effective (S) against Aggregatibacter (microaerophilic) → so it is not "Gram-negative = cipro OK", but rather that oxygen metabolism (aerobic/microaerophilic vs strict anaerobic) determines FQ susceptibility.

One-line note on clarithromycin

  • Correct — macrolides (clarithromycin, especially azithromycin) are effective at killing intracellularly invasive A. actinomycetemcomitans, so they are useful in periodontitis.
  • However, in vitro resistance is variable, so it is more accurate to view them as an adjunct/alternative to the standard amoxicillin (+metronidazole) combination rather than monotherapy.
  • For endocarditis, ceftriaxone is first-line, not a macrolide.

Clinical deep dive: refractory halitosis that responds to only one antibiotic — identifying the culprit

Clinical-observation-based hypotheses. Beyond standard mechanical therapy and oral hygiene, some halitosis patients who did not respond to the usual antibiotics (doxycycline, metronidazole, amoxicillin-clavulanate, ciprofloxacin) respond to only one specific agent. "Which drug works" is a clue for reverse-engineering "which organism is hiding where." Gastric sources such as H. pylori are assumed to have been excluded by separate testing.

Why does only clarithromycin work — intracellular hiding + macrolide pharmacokinetics

Observation: patients in whom doxycycline, metronidazole, amoxicillin-clavulanate, and ciprofloxacin all failed not infrequently respond to clarithromycin.

  • Why the other drugs fail
    • metronidazole: if the culprit is not a strict anaerobe but a microaerophilic/capnophilic Gram-negative, metronidazole is intrinsically ineffective (A. actinomycetemcomitans clinical isolates are essentially all resistant).
    • amoxicillin-clavulanate: if the organism invades and hides inside epithelial cells, β-lactams penetrate cells poorly and cannot reach the intracellular bacteria → failure regardless of in vitro susceptibility.
    • ciprofloxacin: even if susceptible in broth, an organism in biofilm, intracellularly, or deep in the periodontal pocket may not receive adequate sustained concentrations.
  • Why macrolides succeed
    • Massive intracellular accumulation: clarithromycin is actively transported into gingival fibroblasts and epithelial cells (2 µg/mL in medium → ~76 µg/mL inside fibroblasts, exceeding 140 µg/mL at saturation/inflammation). Fibroblasts act as a reservoir with sustained release → hitting the intracellular reservoir that β-lactams cannot reach.
    • Tissue > serum, higher in inflamed tissue: gingival clarithromycin 2.4–3.0 µg/g vs serum 0.5 µg/mL. The drug follows the inflammation (= where the organism lives).
    • In vitro MIC underestimates real efficacy: the achieved intracellular concentration (>100 µg/mL) overwhelms broth resistance values. Even a lab report of "resistant" can be clinically effective against an intracellular organism — a decisive nuance.
    • Anti-inflammatory / anti-biofilm / phagocytosis enhancement: suppresses NF-κB and cytokines, penetrates biofilm, enhances neutrophil killing → dampens the pocket/tongue-coating biofilm ecology itself.
  • Link to the upstream-driver hypothesis: A. actinomycetemcomitans is not a direct malodor producer but an upstream pathogen that destroys and anaerobizes the pocket, creating the environment where the true VSC producers flourish. If clarithromycin removes the intracellular upstream driver and dampens inflammation, the downstream VSC-producing ecology collapses and the halitosis resolves → this explains the paradox that a drug targeting a non-malodor-producing organism cures the malodor.
  • Caveats: there is no guarantee every responder harbors one specific organism. The macrolide's broad intracellular and anti-inflammatory effects may work by suppressing the ecology "without organism specificity."

Campylobacter rectus — an "invasive oral Gram-negative" like Aa

Beyond A. actinomycetemcomitans, a leading candidate that can show the same phenotype (invasive + macrolide-responsive).

  • Identity: an exclusively oral, motile, microaerophilic/anaerobic Gram-negative bacillus. Common in periodontitis lesions (detected in ~80% of periodontitis patients in a large cohort). Tongue-coating microbiome studies of halitosis also report Campylobacter as a significantly enriched genus (consistent with the epidemiologic marker earlier in this document).
  • Invasiveness: it carries ciaB (Campylobacter invasion antigen B), the cell-invasion factor of Campylobacter jejuni → capacity to invade epithelial cells. Moreover, its flagellar motility reaches deep into the periodontal pocket → hard to eliminate with mechanical therapy or non-penetrating antibiotics.
  • Susceptibility: the genus Campylobacter has excellent macrolide (azithromycin/clarithromycin) activity (erythromycin was the traditional first-line), whereas fluoroquinolone (cipro) resistance is rising genus-wide. Periodontal isolates are reported susceptible in broth to tetracycline, metronidazole, penicillin, and cipro (planktonic), but in the invasive/deep-biofilm state, macrolides are favored by the same logic as Aa.
  • Bottom line: a natural extension of the Aa hypothesis. It is reasonable to place Aa together with C. rectus as candidate culprits of "macrolide-only" refractory halitosis.

When only doxycycline works — differential

The rare patient group responding to only doxycycline. "Doxy-only" is the pattern that points to a specific species most weakly (less organism-specific than "clari-only"), so consider these three explanations together.

  1. Doxycycline's own host-modulating (anti-MMP) effect — organism-independent.
    • Tetracyclines inhibit MMPs (collagenase, MMP-8) by a mechanism independent of their antibacterial action (the antibacterial and anti-MMP activities reside on different parts of the molecule). FDA-approved sub-antimicrobial-dose doxycycline (20 mg, Periostat) is used in periodontitis for host inflammatory modulation, not for its antibacterial effect.
    • So "only doxy works" may be less about killing an organism than about dampening tissue destruction/inflammation, shrinking the environment where VSC producers thrive. The fact that none of the other four agents (metro/amox/cipro/clari) has this anti-MMP effect makes this hypothesis especially strong.
  2. A tetracycline-susceptible strain — possibly Aa itself.
    • Doxycycline reaches gingival crevicular fluid (GCF) concentrations above serum (~6 µg/mL, exceeding the MIC90 of periodontal pathogens) and is highly lipophilic, penetrating cells and biofilm.
    • Tetracycline was the historical first-line for localized aggressive periodontitis (Aa) (Aa is highly susceptible). A "doxy-only" responder may harbor a strain resistant to the others but tetracycline-susceptible, which could be a macrolide-resistant/tetracycline-susceptible strain of Aa rather than mycoplasma.
    • Key point: which drug works depends more on the strain's resistance profile than on the species — the same Aa can present as a clari-responder or a doxy-responder depending on strain.
  3. Mycoplasma salivarium.
    • An oral commensal mycoplasma residing in dental plaque and the gingival sulcus, associated with periodontal disease (promoting inflammatory-cell infiltration). Having no cell wall, it is intrinsically β-lactam/metronidazole-resistant, and oral isolates frequently show macrolide (erythromycin, 23S rRNA 2057A) and fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin 25.7%) resistance while remaining tetracycline-susceptible → fits the "doxy-only" mirror profile.
    • But three weaknesses: ① it is not a direct VSC producer — its metabolism is glucose fermentation + arginine→ammonia, with no evidence of sulfur-compound production, so any role is likely indirect/upstream like Aa; ② it is essentially unconfirmable because routine culture misses it (special media needed); ③ tetracycline resistance also exists (~34%), so it is not absolute.

Deep dive: VSC (volatile sulfur compound)-producing bacteria — the causative organisms of halitosis

What VSCs are — the chemical reality of halitosis

  • VSC (Volatile Sulfur Compounds) = the main culprit (80–90%) of oral malodor. Three are key.
  • Hydrogen sulfide H₂S — rotten-egg smell. Mainly produced from L-cysteine degradation.
  • Methyl mercaptan CH₃SH — rotten-cabbage/periodontal-pus smell. Mainly produced from L-methionine degradation. A malodor marker of periodontal disease — the higher the CH₃SH/H₂S ratio, the more severe the periodontitis.
  • Dimethyl sulfide (CH₃)₂S — relatively larger contribution in systemic (blood-borne) halitosis.
  • Mechanism: anaerobic Gram-negative bacteria degrade sulfur-containing amino acids using desulfhydrating enzymes (cysteine desulfhydrase, methionine-γ-lyase/mgl) → releasing VSC. The main production sites are the posterior tongue (tongue coating) and periodontal pockets.
  • Study data: H₂S (~90–100 ppb)·CH₃SH in gingivitis/periodontitis patients are significantly higher than in the healthy group (H₂S ~15 ppb). Halitosis prevalence: healthy 3% vs gingivitis 39.5% vs periodontitis 42.9%.

Porphyromonas gingivalis deep dive — a key VSC/periodontitis pathogen

  • Identity: Gram-negative anaerobic bacillus, Bacteroidota (formerly Bacteroidetes), black-pigmented colonies. One of this document's 5 representative GNAB species (see section 4 above).
  • Core of the red complex: together with T. denticola·T. forsythia, the 3-organism complex of chronic periodontitis (red complex). Dominant in deep periodontal pockets and mature plaque.
  • Especially strong VSC production: using methionine-γ-lyase (mgl), it produces large amounts of CH₃SH from methionine and H₂S from cysteine → the main culprit of the characteristic periodontal-pus malodor. Large H₂S·CH₃SH also confirmed in oral isolates from animals (dogs).
  • Virulence: tissue invasion and immune evasion via gingipains (proteases)·LPS·fimbriae. As a "keystone pathogen", it collapses periodontal microbial balance.
  • Systemic associations: periodontitis → active research on links to atherosclerosis·rheumatoid arthritis·Alzheimer's (reported brain gingipain detection), etc.

Foods that serve as substrates for VSC production

VSCs are ultimately made by bacteria degrading sulfur-containing amino acids/sulfur compounds. Therefore, eating foods that are sulfur sources increases the substrate and worsens halitosis.

⚠️ Key principle — "direct volatilization vs bacterial substrate" are not mutually exclusive Plant sulfur foods like garlic, onion, and cabbage serve both as "direct volatilization on chewing" and as "bacterial substrate after swallowing". The only part that smells purely without bacteria is a tiny fraction that is absorbed and exhaled through the lungs (AMS), and even that involves bacteria in its production. It is not only animal protein (cysteine·methionine) that is a bacterial substrate — the plant-based organosulfur of alliums, cruciferous vegetables, and leafy greens all become bacterial substrates too. See both ① (animal) and ② (plant) below.

Broadly divided into ① animal sulfur substrates, ② plant sulfur substrates (both direct volatilization + bacterial substrate), and ③ environment-shaping (indirect).

① Animal/high-protein sulfur substrates = foods rich in sulfur-containing amino acids (cysteine·methionine)

Because bacteria degrade cysteine→H₂S, methionine→CH₃SH, high-protein foods rich in these amino acids are the key substrate. Quantitatively the largest source of the body's total H₂S (see deep dive D above).

That is, it is not a food that "emits a sulfur smell on its own". - Lettuce's main sulfur = a "sulfur-sugar" called sulfoquinovose (SQ): the sulfur in leafy greens is not inorganic sulfate but, quantitatively the largest, the SQ (6-deoxy-6-sulfo-D-glucose) head group of the chloroplast thylakoid membrane lipid sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol (SQDG). Desulfovibrio is a side player. That is, the lead of the lettuce SQ→H₂S pathway is Bilophila wadsworthia, not Desulfovibrio. - Summary: lettuce's sulfur is neither the volatile organosulfur of garlic/cruciferous nor simple inorganic sulfate — quantitatively and in the literature, the core is SQ (derived from a sulfur-sugar lipid). This SQ converges to H₂S in the colon through polymicrobial cooperation (E. rectale fermentation → Bilophila desulfonation).

In discussions of halitosis/gut health, the question "which bacterium makes the most hydrogen sulfide?" has a different answer depending on which substrate, at which site, and whether cell counts are considered. D. ★Quantitative total reassessment — "H₂S total-amount leader = SRB" is an old view

  • Textbook orthodoxy: the No.1 producer of the body's total H₂S is sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB, Desulfovibrio). ← an outdated simplification.
  • Recent reversal (quantitative): the capacity for cysteine degradation (pathway①) is virtually ubiquitous across all gut bacteria, and multiplying by cell count·universality, the cysteine desulfhydration pathway dominates the gut's total H₂S. SRB is low-density and limited in distribution, so in total amount it is a supporting player.
  • Basis: "The Capacity to Produce H₂S via Cysteine Degradation Is Ubiquitous in the Human Gut Microbiome" (Front Microbiol 2021; PMC8564485).
    • Among 4,644 UHGG genomes, cysteine-degradation genes are broadly present.
    • 100% detected in 6,623 healthy individuals, 100% expressed in 736 metatranscriptome samples — common across healthy·IBD·CRC.
    • Cell counts·expression of cysteine-degrading bacteria overwhelm SRB.
    • (supporting: bioRxiv 2021.03.24.436781, HAL hal-02627096)
  • Corrected conclusion:
  • ❌ "body-wide H₂S leader = Desulfovibrio (SRB)" — old view.
  • ✅ quantitative total leader = the cysteine-degradation pathway (a broad gut-bacterial population). SRB is local/supporting.
  • ✅ The largest quantitative H₂S source is the cysteine of animal protein — this actually reinforces the Neo-Skepticism thesis of "sulfur intake → H₂S → gut/systemic effects" (high protein is quantitatively larger than leafy-green SQ).
  • Fusobacterium·P. gingivalis are merely the representatives of the oral cavity/specific pathologies, not a single "star organism". The answer for gut total amount is the cysteine-degrading gut-bacterial population.

E. One-line summary

By site, oral = Fusobacterium nucleatum, gut (old view) = SRB. But the body-wide quantitative total is dominated by the cysteine-degradation pathway (ubiquitous gut bacteria) (Front Microbiol 2021), with SRB as a supporting player. Leafy greens like lettuce follow a separate relay pathway of SQ→(E. rectale fermentation → Bilophila desulfonation)→H₂S.

rifaximin — "broad anaerobic coverage that works only inside the gut"

  • Non-absorbable rifamycin class — even taken orally, systemic absorption <0.4%; the drug stays in the intestinal lumen. Inhibits RNA polymerase.
  • 4-class perspective: broadly covers aerobes·anaerobes, Gram-positive·negative → touches all of GPAB·GNAB·GPAC·GNAC. The key is that it simultaneously hits GPAB (most dominant) and GNAB (2nd), which make up most of gut overgrowth.
  • Why it's first-line for SIBO:
  • Low systemic side effects·resistance pressure (luminal action), relatively low C. difficile risk.
  • Especially effective for hydrogen (H₂) SIBO — meta-analyses show eradication·symptom improvement.
  • For the methane type (IMO, involving Methanobrevibacter), low efficacy alone → combine with neomycin (or metronidazole).
  • Limitations: fundamentally a temporary biomass reduction; recurs without correcting diet·motility (MMC)·underlying disease. Regrows if bile·SQ·cysteine substrates keep coming in.

ciprofloxacin — powerless against anaerobes, but a legitimate target for "classic coliform SIBO"

  • Fluoroquinolone (FQ), inhibits DNA gyrase/topo IV. Broad systemic absorption·tissue distribution (the opposite of rifaximin).
  • 4-class perspective (anaerobes): essentially resistant (R) to all anaerobes (GPAB·GNAB·GPAC·GNAC). The principle repeated throughout this document — FQs are unsuitable for pure anaerobic infections such as halitosis/periodontal organisms. This part is exactly correct.
  • Where cipro works well: facultatively anaerobic·aerobic Gram-negative Enterobacteriaceae (E. coli·Klebsiella·Enterobacter etc.)·some Gram-negative bacilli. Representative targets include urinary·intra-abdominal·biliary tract·traveler's diarrhea (enterotoxigenic E. coli).
  • ⚠️ Important correction — don't assume SIBO is "overwhelmingly anaerobic": SIBO's bacterial composition looks opposite depending on the definition method.
  • Classic SIBO defined by culture (jejunal aspirate culture) is instead dominated by Gram-negative aerobic "coliforms". The standard textbook/guideline definition itself is "coliforms (Gram-negative aerobes) abnormally increased in the small intestine" (ACG·Merck: "predominantly gram-negative bacteria that ferment carbohydrates"), and the most dominant species in aspirate culture are repeatedly reported as Escherichia coli·Klebsiella pneumoniae·Enterococcus·Streptococcus viridans·Pseudomonas aeruginosa (Gutierrez 2012). In Choung 2011 (jejunal aspirate, n=675), it was aerobes 83.3% vs anaerobes 4.6%, overwhelmingly aerobic Gram-negatives.
  • On the other hand, SIBO/IMO defined by breath testing (H₂/CH₄) highlights the contribution of fermentative·hydrogen-producing anaerobes·archaea, so it "looks anaerobe-dominant". That is, "SIBO = anaerobic GPAB/GNAB dominance" is only one facet from the breath-test perspective; from the culture perspective, the Gram-negative aerobic coliforms that cipro precisely targets are the leads.
  • The actual position of cipro/norfloxacin historically·evidentially:
  • Before rifaximin appeared, the standard SIBO treatment was in fact norfloxacin·ciprofloxacin·amoxicillin-clavulanate. A systemic FQ targeting coliforms was the logical·empirical first-line.
  • In comparative trials, the eradication rate of ciprofloxacin/norfloxacin is broadly the same range as rifaximin (Chilean IBS-SIBO study: cipro 53% vs rifaximin 59% eradication), so it is not a "miss" but a valid alternative. Only in the synthesis of head-to-head meta-analyses is rifaximin slightly superior with better safety, which is why it was pushed to first-line.

Key contrast — why rifaximin is first-line and cipro is a lower-priority alternative

  • The identity of gut overgrowth depends on the "definition method": by culture criteria, Gram-negative aerobic coliforms (E. coli·Klebsiella etc.) dominate; by breath-test (H₂/CH₄) criteria, the contribution of fermentative anaerobes·archaea is highlighted. "Overwhelmingly anaerobic" is a half-truth.
  • rifaximin = a broad-spectrum drug that suppresses anaerobes·aerobes, Gram± broadly at the luminal level → covers most under either definition, low systemic toxicity → SIBO first-line.
  • ciprofloxacin = can't catch anaerobes (R) but precisely targets Gram-negative aerobic coliforms → actually effective (eradication rate in a similar range to rifaximin) for culture-dominant coliform SIBO, but ① leaves the anaerobic component in mixed types and ② carries systemic FQ toxicity (tendon rupture·QT·CNS·aortic aneurysm·C. difficile) → a lower-priority alternative.
  • Ultimately, rifaximin wins not because "cipro misses the target" but because its coverage is broader and its systemic safety superior. Cipro is legitimate as a coliform-targeting drug and remains a reasonable alternative when rifaximin is unresponsive·unavailable.

Additional clinical points - Methane type (IMO): archaea (Methanobrevibacter smithii) are not bacteria and are outside the 4 classes — rifaximin+neomycin combination is customary. - Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) SIBO: linked to the cysteine-degradation ubiquity·SRB of deep dive D above. Rifaximin reduces anaerobic biomass and thus lowers H₂S, but persistence requires concurrent substrate management (high protein·SQ·bile taurine). - Fungal overgrowth (SIFO): antifungals separately — antibiotics (both) ineffective.

Deep dive — Clostridioides difficile infection (CDI): a balanced view of outpatient antibiotic risk

CDI is the most feared antibiotic complication, but in low-risk outpatients on a single course the absolute risk is low, so blanket fear is disproportionate. Caution belongs not to "all antibiotics, all patients" but to high-risk antibiotics × high-risk patients.

The absolute risk is low (numbers)

  • Large US cohort (2008–2020): among 36.6 million patients on outpatient antibiotics, community-associated CDI (CA-CDI) was 0.03% (~3 per 10,000); even the worst (clindamycin) is ~1 per 1,000.
  • Number needed to harm (NNH): even the highest-risk agents are ~1 per 300 prescriptions. Doxycycline is the lowest-risk reference, and switching from doxycycline to a high-risk agent causes one extra CDI only per ~1,164 (clindamycin) to ~1,828 (fluoroquinolones) — i.e., even the excess risk over a low-risk drug is small in absolute terms.

Where caution belongs — high-risk antibiotics × high-risk patients

  • High-risk antibiotics: clindamycin ≫ later-generation cephalosporins (cefdinir, cefuroxime, etc.) and amoxicillin-clavulanate > fluoroquinolones. (Low-risk: doxycycline, narrow penicillins, macrolides, TMP-SMX, nitrofurantoin.)
  • High-risk patients: age >65, recent hospitalization/healthcare exposure, PPIs, prior CDI, immunosuppression, IBD. (CDI can recur or become severe when it occurs.)

Deep dive — relationship with neurodegenerative diseases (Parkinson's·Alzheimer's)

Evidence is accumulating that oral·gut anaerobes contribute to neurodegeneration via the gut–brain axis and the oral–brain axis. The 3 key genera — Porphyromonas gingivalis (Bacteroidota, GNAB), Fusobacterium nucleatum (Fusobacteriota, GNAB), Desulfovibrio (Thermodesulfobacteriota, GNAB) — all belong to this document's GNAB (Gram-negative anaerobic bacilli) group and contribute to Alzheimer's disease (AD)·Parkinson's disease (PD) through different pathways.

1) Porphyromonas gingivalis → Alzheimer's disease (oral–brain axis, gingipain hypothesis)

  • Key finding: Dominy et al. (2019, Science Advances 5:eaau3333) detected P. gingivalis and its toxic protease gingipains (Kgp·RgpA·RgpB) in postmortem AD brains, showing that gingipain concentration correlated with tau·ubiquitin pathology. Multiple reports confirmed gingipains in 90–100% of AD brains.
  • Mechanism:
  • P. gingivalis oral infection → mouse brain colonization → increased amyloid-β (Aβ1–42) production (hypothesis that Aβ, as an antimicrobial peptide, may be a defense response to infection).
  • Gingipains cleave·hyperphosphorylate tau → promoting neurofibrillary tangle formation.
  • Chronic neuroinflammation from LPS·OMVs (outer membrane vesicles).
  • Therapeutic attempt: a small-molecule gingipain inhibitor (COR388/atuzaginstat) reduced brain colonization·Aβ1–42 production·neuroinflammation and protected hippocampal neurons in mice → advanced to human trials (basis for the hypothesis that infection is upstream of the other pathologies).

2) Fusobacterium nucleatum → Alzheimer's disease (neuroinflammation·oral–gut–brain axis)

  • Epidemiology: F. nucleatum is a representative bridging organism that overgrows in periodontitis and is detected in the serum of AD·cognitive-impairment patients.
  • Animal evidence: F. nucleatum-induced periodontitis caused worsened cognitive impairment·Aβ accumulation·increased tau phosphorylation in 5XFAD mice (Frontiers Aging Neurosci 2022;14:912709). Oral infection contributes to cognitive/brain-pathology abnormalities in AD-like rats and alters gut microbiota composition·metabolism (PMID 36188448).
  • Mechanism: induction of systemic·neuroinflammation, indirect effects via gut microbial dysbiosis. A separate axis from F. nucleatum's contribution to colorectal cancer (FadA·Fap2 adhesins) discussed in this document, but sharing the common pattern of "periodontal lesion → systemic dissemination → distant-organ pathology".

3) Desulfovibrio → Parkinson's disease (hydrogen sulfide·α-synuclein aggregation)

  • Epidemiology: Murros et al. (2021, Front Cell Infect Microbiol 11:652617) detected Desulfovibrio in the stool of all 20 PD patients, present at higher levels than healthy controls and more abundant with more severe PD.
  • Mechanism:
  • Desulfovibrio, a sulfate-reducing bacterium (SRB), produces hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) and LPS, and some strains synthesize magnetite — all of which can induce oligomerization·aggregation of α-synuclein.
  • PD's α-synuclein aggregates (Lewy bodies) accumulate not only in the brain but also in the digestive tract·autonomic nervous system, and the hypothesis is that gut-formed aggregates propagate to the brain along the vagus nerve in a prion-like manner (consistent with the Braak hypothesis).
  • Huynh et al. (2023, Front Cell Infect Microbiol 13:1181315): Desulfovibrio strains isolated from PD-patient stool induced larger and more numerous α-synuclein aggregates than controls (spouse stool) in a C. elegans model — the pathogenicity difference may be related to H₂S-producing capacity.
  • Connection to this document: among the 3 H₂S pathways covered in deep dive D (cysteine desulfhydrase / SQ sulfoglycolysis / dsr sulfate reduction), Desulfovibrio's dsr sulfate reduction is pointed to as the main source of PD-related H₂S. This is the junction where the VSC (halitosis) and SRB axes extend even to neurodegeneration.

One-line summary: all three are GNAB (Gram-negative anaerobic bacilli); P. gingivalis·F. nucleatum contribute to neurodegeneration via oral lesion → AD, and Desulfovibrio via gut H₂S → PD — two axes. This is why oral hygiene and gut sulfur-metabolism management connect to neurological health.

Deep dive — psychiatric disorders and the microbiome (gut–brain axis)

The gut–brain axis: gut microbes influence the brain and behavior. Among psychiatric conditions the strongest evidence is for depression/anxiety — specific microbial signatures replicate across large cohorts and the phenotype transfers by FMT in animals.

The gut–brain axis — how the gut talks to the brain (4 pathways)

  • Neural (vagus nerve): the vagus is the key relay; enteroendocrine "neuropod" cells signal it directly. In mice, vagotomy blocks the depression phenotype transferred by FMT → causal evidence for the vagal route.
  • Metabolites: SCFAs (e.g., butyrate), neurotransmitters (GABA, serotonin precursors), tryptophan–kynurenine, DOPAC (a dopamine metabolite).
  • Immune: cytokines, systemic inflammation, gut barrier permeability.
  • Endocrine: HPA axis and cortisol.

Depression / anxiety

  • Large human observation + replication: Valles-Colomer 2019 Nat Microbiol — Flemish Gut Flora Project (n=1,054) replicated in LifeLines DEEP (n=1,063). Coprococcus and Dialister depleted in depression (even after adjusting for antidepressants); butyrate producers Faecalibacterium and Coprococcus correlate with higher quality of life. Microbial capacity to make DOPAC (a dopamine metabolite) ↔ better mental quality of life.
  • Causal support in mice: the depression phenotype transfers from human to mouse via FMT and is blocked by vagotomy. Anxiety shows reproducible microbiome signatures co-occurring with depression.

★ Eggerthella and depression — the most consistent bacterial marker

Species/genus-level findings in depression microbiome studies are largely inconsistent, but Eggerthella is the exception — the most reproducible signature.

  • Consistency: in one systematic review, every study that reported Eggerthella (12/12) found it increased in psychiatric cases. A 44-study meta-analysis (2,091 patients / 2,792 controls) likewise found it consistently enriched as a pro-inflammatory taxon despite overall heterogeneity. Elevated across MDD, bipolar, and schizophrenia, and positively correlated with depression/anxiety/stress scores.
  • Mechanistic hypothesis ① tryptophan–kynurenine shunt: in three multi-omics studies, higher Eggerthella correlated with higher kynurenine and lower tryptophan — i.e., tryptophan diverted from serotonin (5-HT) synthesis toward the neurotoxic kynurenine pathway. The gut makes ~95% of the body's serotonin, tied to tryptophan availability → a plausible depression-relevant mechanism.
  • Mechanistic hypothesis ② pro-inflammatory / bile acids: Eggerthella (Eggerthellaceae) is a pro-inflammatory taxon and a bile-acid/steroid metabolizer. Bile acids and their receptors (FXR·TGR5) modulate neuroinflammation, gut–brain signaling, and neuroplasticity in depression.
  • Antidepressant-response prediction: non-responders have higher baseline Eggerthellaceae (significantly lower in responders). This fits the idea that microbial disruption of the tryptophan–serotonin pathway blunts SSRI efficacy → a candidate treatment-response biomarker. (Consistent with E. lenta being a potent drug-metabolizer of digoxin and levodopa — see the GPAB box above.)
  • Causality / caveats: association-level. Some Mendelian-randomization analyses hint at a causal link to MDD (needs validation); reverse causation and confounders (diet, medication) remain possible. Being "the most consistent marker" does not equal causation.

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) — the case for a link

(A collection of the main evidence supporting a real ASD–microbiome association.)

  • Large multi-kingdom signature — Su 2024, Nat Microbiol: metagenomics of 1,627 children found changes not only in bacteria but in archaea, fungi, viruses, microbial genes, and metabolic pathways. A 31-marker multi-kingdom/functional panel distinguished ASD with AUC 0.91 (vs 0.68–0.87 for single-kingdom) → a possible non-invasive diagnostic.
  • Causal transfer in mice — Sharon 2019, Cell: transferring ASD-child stool to germ-free mice produced ASD-like behaviors and altered splicing of ASD-related genes in the brain. Metabolites reduced in ASD — 5-aminovaleric acid and taurine (GABA-A agonists) — improved behavior in BTBR mice when restored.
  • Long-term microbiota transfer therapy (MTT) — Kang·Adams, Sci Rep 2019: at 2-year follow-up of an open-label trial, GI symptoms ~58% lower and core autism symptoms ~50% lower (expert-rated), with persistent microbiota changes (Bifidobacterium·Prevotella).
  • Mechanisms: ASD-donor FMT modulates tryptophan–serotonin and vitamin-B6 metabolism and behavior in mice; microbial metabolites such as propionic acid affect the excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) balance.

Deep dive — deconstructing the "more colon diversity is better" myth

In the colon, diversity that is too low genuinely is a problem (colonization resistance, resilience). But there is no evidence that "above a certain level, more is better." "Low diversity = disease" is only partly true, and "high diversity = health" is an overstated, commercialized frame.

Too low IS a problem — the nature of the colon

  • The colon is a high-biomass, open ecosystem, so low diversity weakens colonization resistance → vulnerability to pathogens such as C. difficile and reduced resilience.
  • Mechanism: a diverse community pre-empts the nutrients a pathogen needs (nutrient blocking), blocking invasion (Science 2024). Diversity loss is reproducible in active IBD, recurrent C. difficile, and after broad-spectrum antibiotics.

But there is no evidence that "above a threshold, higher is better" — plateau

  • There is no upper "this-much-is-healthy" cutoff. No validated threshold says higher diversity means healthier.
  • Colonization resistance saturates once functional coverage is "enough." What matters is not the raw number but functional redundancy covering the pathogen's nutrient niches → once niches are covered, adding rare taxa adds little benefit (diminishing returns).
  • Shannon diversity is non-linear, maximized at a specific Bacteroidetes/Firmicutes ratio (~15%/80%) → not "more is better" but an optimal range; "excessive diversity can also be a problem."
  • Functional redundancy means even a "minimum healthy diversity" can't be pinned to a number → a U-shaped / saturating curve: too low is bad, but above a point, higher is not better.

Counterexamples within the colon + a crude metric

  • Colorectal cancer (CRC): tumors are enriched in oral pathobionts (Fusobacterium·Parvimonas, etc.), yet fecal alpha-diversity often does not differ significantly from controls (beta-dispersion is higher) → the diversity number cannot flag cancer.
  • Parkinson's disease: alpha-diversity is inconsistent, with some studies reporting higher richness (more rare taxa). "Alpha-diversity is not a marker of PD/MS."
  • Association ≠ causation: mostly cross-sectional. Even for IBD it is unresolved whether dysbiosis is cause or consequence. Diversity is driven more by diet (Westernization) than by disease, and all non-genetic factors explain only ~16% of the variance.

Aside: the rise and fall of "F/B ratio = obesity marker" (a textbook oversimplification)

  • Rise: in 2006, Ley·Turnbaugh·Gordon (Nature), from mice and a small human study, reported that obese subjects had more Firmicutes and less Bacteroidetes (higher F/B ratio), with the reverse in lean subjects → the catchy claim that "one F/B ratio tells you about obesity and gut health" spread widely and was cited across diet, probiotic, and testing marketing.
  • Fall: larger studies and meta-analyses failed to reproduce it. One meta-analysis found no signal in the F/B ratio to distinguish obese from normal weight (data clustered by study/batch, not BMI); a 2020 review concluded the F/B ratio has no validity as an obesity biomarker (lifestyle confounders + methodological noise).
  • Why it collapsed: ① phylum is far too coarse — each phylum lumps thousands of species with opposite functions, so a single ratio loses the information; ② huge inter-individual variability; ③ heavily affected by sequencing/primers/taxonomy; ④ correlation, not causation. (Bonus: a 2021 reclassification even renamed them Firmicutes→Bacillota, Bacteroidetes→Bacteroidota.)
  • Lesson: a textbook case of reducing gut health to one catchy number (F/B ratio, diversity score) — widely believed by the public but overturned by science. The "diversity score" is the same trap.

The real "gaslighting" — commercial tests

  • Direct-to-consumer microbiome tests are not clinically validated and there is no established diagnostic standard.
  • The same sample is rated "excellent," "average," or "unfavorable" by different companies — "high/low" is judged against the company's own database, not a scientific baseline. Many companies then sell their own probiotics based on the result (conflict of interest). International consensus: limited clinical utility.
  • Bottom line: a "diversity score = health report card" has never been validated. What matters is not the number but ① what grows (composition, keystones, pathobionts) ② what function (colonization resistance, butyrate vs H₂S/toxins) ③ context (diet, site).
